India's Tobacco Tax Reform 1 / 5
2026 Reform
India's tobacco tax just got its biggest overhaul in a decade.
From 1 February 2026, India raised cigarette GST from 28% to 40%, plus reimposed excise duty.

This is genuine progress. It is also incomplete.

Here is the honest assessment.
Central Tax (Rate) Notification No. 19/2025
The New Structure
Total tax burden: ~52–53% of retail price.
40%
GST (up from 28%)
Highest rate in India's GST structure
₹2–8.5k
Excise / 1,000 sticks
Reimposed after a 7-year freeze
A stick at ₹18 will now cost ~₹21–22. Plus: RSP-based valuation for smokeless tobacco closes the under-reporting loophole.
Source: Central Excise (Amendment) Act, 2025
What It Gets Right
Three genuine wins in this reform.
✓ Price freeze broken, 7 years of real price decline corrected in one move.
✓ First earmarking, HSSNS Cess is India's first legislative earmarking of tobacco revenue for health.
✓ Evasion closed, Capacity-based cess ties liability to production, not declared sales.
Author analysis · Central Excise Act 2025
The Bidi Paradox
The most harmful product just got a tax cut.
40%
Cigarette GST
Less harmful. Urban. Higher income users.
18%
Bidi GST (down from 28%)
More harmful. Rural. Poorest 20%.
Bidi smoking = 2.87x higher asthma risk vs 1.82x for cigarettes. 2.6x higher TB mortality risk.

Taxing bidis at cigarette-equivalent rates could avert up to 695,159 years of life lost.
Jain et al. (Nicotine & Tobacco Research, 2024)
Net Assessment
Right direction. Insufficient distance.
Achieved
Higher tax · First earmarking · Evasion compliance
Still Needed
WHO 75% threshold not met · Bidi tax reduced · No independent governance

Economic Policy is Health Policy 1 / 4
Policy
Every economic policy is a health policy. We just pretend it's not.
We say "health" and think: hospitals, doctors, medicines.

But most of what determines health happens long before someone reaches a clinic.

Food access, wage security, housing, social protection, these have greater bearing on life expectancy than any pill ever will.
The Siloed System
We treat health as a sector. It's actually an outcome.
Anaemia isn't just nutrition
It's food prices and the burden of unpaid care work.
Heatstroke deaths aren't just medical
They reveal failures in water access, housing, and labour protection.
Maternal deaths
Linked to inadequate transport, gender norms, and underpaid frontline workers.
These aren't exceptions. They're the system working exactly as designed: in silos.
Why It Matters
Health is where every policy accumulates.
Good or bad. Intended or not.

💰
Pensions lapse → declining mental health
👩
Unpaid care work → women's health suffers
🌿
Underfunded nutrition programmes → collapse
🏙️
Ignored urban policy → infectious disease spreads
Policy is upstream. Health is downstream. But the circle always closes.
A New Question
Stop asking "Is this a health policy?" Start asking,
"What does this policy do to people's health?"
If a policy increases hunger, precarity, or burnout, is it really development?

And if it protects rest, food, care, and autonomy, isn't that healthcare too?
What policy do you think shapes health the most, even though no one calls it that? 💬
We Say "Vulnerable Women" 1 / 4
Gender & Health
We say "vulnerable women."
But it's the systems that are weak.
"Vulnerability" isn't innate.

It's constructed, through low pay, unsafe housing, unpaid care, and systemic neglect.
The Language Problem
Policy language shapes funding, programmes, and action.
When we frame women, migrants, or the poor as "vulnerable" without asking why, we shift the lens:
From structural causes
→
To individual traits
From accountability
→
To sympathy
It lets broken systems off the hook.
What Needs to Change
Design for justice. Not charity.
01 Replace deficit framing with structural analysis
02 Design with dignity and justice, not pity
03 Make systems accountable, not just individuals "resilient"
Vulnerability is not the problem. Power is.
A New Frame
What if we rewrote our policies starting with accountability, not pity?
Ask yourself:

Who made someone vulnerable?

What would it take to stop that from happening again?
Language matters. The words we choose in policy determine who we hold accountable, and who we let off the hook. 💬
India's Health Insurance Gaps 1 / 4
Health Finance
Why India's health insurance still leaves many behind.
70%
healthcare costs still out-of-pocket
500M+
PM-JAY cards issued
₹3K+
spent per minute OOP by Indians
The paradox: massive coverage on paper. Persistent catastrophic expenditure in reality.
Source: National Health Accounts 2021–22; PM-JAY Factsheet 2024; WHO GHED 2022
The Illusion
₹5 lakh cover. But what does it actually cover?
Promise
Free ₹5 lakh inpatient cover under Ayushman Bharat for hospitalisation expenses.
Pitfall
No coverage for OPD visits, diagnostic tests, or medicines, where most spending actually happens.
70%
of total health spending still comes out-of-pocket, even with insurance
Source: PM-JAY website; WHO Global Health Expenditure Database
Rural & Gender Gaps
The card exists. The care doesn't always.
Average distance to an empanelled hospital in rural areas: 100 km.
30% of eligible rural families never use their PM-JAY card despite having one.
In Rajasthan, women are only 45% of hospitalisations under Bhamashah, despite being half the population.
400M+ Indians sit in the "missing middle", too rich for PM-JAY, too poor for private insurance.
Source: PHFI survey 2023; NFHS-5; World Bank India Poverty Report 2023
Three Bold Moves
What would actually fix this.
01Extend Ayushman Bharat to cover OPD, medicines, and diagnostics, where the real spending happens.
02Strict audits, penalise fraud, de-empanel facilities that deny care or game claims.
03Design a middle-tier insurance model for the 400M+ caught between public and private, and auto-enroll eligible women.
Insurance is only as good as the system behind it. Beyond the card, we need care. 💬
